Overview

Hardware-based sensing systems are essential for collecting activity, communications, and health data during research projects. Despite their importance, traditional publications rarely warrant extended discussion on design processes or methods.

For over a decade, researchers from Georgia Tech have built and deployed bespoke systems in real-world environments. These devices have been baked in the sun, submerged in the ocean, chewed by muskrats, and bashed by otters. Workshop Topic Areas

In this workshop, members of the research community will share their unique field-deployed/deployable hardware systems, processes for designing solutions to the unique constraints their systems face, and their lessons learned from failed prototypes. This workshop will distill multidisciplinary information from across the Ubicomp and ISWC communities to expand the knowledge and capabilities of hardware developers working at research-scale. While the majority of our programming will draw from accepted 1-page extended abstract submissions, we will welcome attendance from the entire Ubicomp/ISWC community as an open-workshop.

  • Research Scale Prototyping: Techniques for low-volume, budget-sensitive development.
  • Multidisciplinary Usability: Designing for unique user groups including animals and scientists.
  • Ruggedization: Building for extreme environments and destructive use cases.
  • Accessibility: Strategies for affordability and reproducibility.
  • Hardware on Soft Users: Making wearables for nontraditional users and tasks.
  • Scalable Leave-behind Systems: Designing long-term remote deployments.
  • Communities of Practice: Strengthening field hardware research communities.

Workshop Format

  • Lightning Talks: Fast-paced 7-minute talks with rich visuals. (~4 rounds)
  • Breakout Discussions: Small group discussions around presenter topics + demos.
  • Field Guide Zine: Collaborative creation of a visual, physical zine capturing key insights and systems.
